Cannonballs

Cannonballs are ammunition for cannons, a form of artillery. It is a solid sphereical projectile.
The Cast Iron cannonball was first designed and produced by a French Artillery Engineer.
When fired on the battlefield, the cannonball would fly through the enemy ranks, causing collateral casualties.
The cannon is used for artillery attacks on formations of troops, structures, naval vessels, infantry, and heavily defended positions.
The cannonball is no longer used in modern combat and has been completely replaced by the more modern explosive shells.



The cannonball will sometimes be heated up to attack objectives that are susceptible to heat damage, such as wooden structures and ships.
Although no longer used in combat, they can still be fired for ceremonies and other occasions.
Cannonballs can be made of dressed stone, but were made of iron after the 17th century.
Cannonballs were also sometimes explosive, and packed with gunpowder.
Cannonballs also could be packed with solid iron projectiles (small iron items that could be shot out when the cannonball reaches the desired range) and cut down a large number of concentrated troops, like a grenade.

TRIVIA:

Cannons are used for:

  1. Attack on formations of troops.
  2. Attack on structures.
  3. Attack on naval vessels.
  4. Attack on Infantry.
  5. Attack on heavily defended positions, e.g, a castle.

Cannons can be:


  1. Heated
  2. Made with dressed stone
  3. Made with iron
  4. Packed with gunpowder
  5. Packed with solid iron projectiles
